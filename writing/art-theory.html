Art as I understand it art's definition Art is whatever people say it is. Someone can get caught up in their own ideas and state it as though the entirety of it should be viewed from that perspective. To say nothing about whether they are necessarily right or not, regardless of how strongly someone insists art should or shouldn't be seen others will have their own opinions. As David Hume put it: "The difference, it is said, is very wide between judgement and sentiment. All sentiment is right; because sentiment has a reference to nothing beyond itself, and is always real, wherever a man is conscious of it." In other words, if someone is getting something out of a work of art, that "sentiment" is present self-evidently, no matter how substantiated or not their feeling is. It could be broadly said that art is an arrangement of elements in space and/or time for non-practical purpose but that does not encompass the entirety of how art is/has been percieved. In short though, any philosophy that has ever even suggested a modicum of consensus has been called in question by someone else. Even something like "art is something that you observe and think something about" is at least played around with in institutional artwork--"art" that's only seen as such because of the context around it--such as mundane objects featured in an art gallery. Morris Weitz summed it up by calling art an "open concept" whose "conditions of application are emendable and corrigible". Although the debate over its validity continues I think it can more or less be left at that. Art is a thing that has been known to occur at least once or twice[citation needed]. history and future In any case, a long time ago people started doing something that some people might call art. We might even say that there is a clear correlation between the beliefs and understanding of people and the art they have made. Prior to the Italian Renaissance, artists were generally seen as laborers in the same vein as any general craft-maker. Sculptures and paintings do serve a utility in a decorative sense; a beautiful room is emotionally more comfortable to sit in. And especially decorated pottery and other such appliances. The line becomes hazy between art and craft even now, and so it's easy to imagine how that perception was made. A pervading notion of God(s) also diminished the human's role in creation. Perhaps given the belief that gods had created the world and everything in it, the role of 'creator' was exclusive to the supernatural: a human could only channel divine inspiration. Regardless, this changed during the Italian Renaissance. This was most likely owed more to the rising of Humanism more than anything else, a philosophy that took agency from gods, emphasizing the powers of human beings as well as accordingly shifting away from dogma. What was groundbreaking hardened into a stagnant formula embodied by the Paris Salon jury, the overseers of the most significant art exhibition by far at the time. This came to a head when it refused Manet and other impressionist painters entry, leading to the 1863 Salon des Refusés, a separate exhibit for the artists turned away from the official Salon. The basic theme to work exhibited in this new Salon was content that broke from the formulaicness of the official exhibit, mostly by playing at the edges of what was considered appropriate subject matter for the public eye. This was indicative of the relaxing of societal norms and further changes to the status quo. As the Impressionists broke further from the refined techniques of the Salon style, a parallel trend was occurring in music although key figures such as Debussy and Ravel did not like the term being applied to their work. Finally, the effect of the first world war deeply impacted people's view and shattered conceptions of virtue or morality being a universality. God was dead, as Nietzsche had declared some decades prior. The Dadaist movement developed in reaction as well as the atonal movement in music, both expressors of dissonance and a lack of directionality. In short, it's clear that the art of society has always reflected its values and that technological progress has a strong bearing on new developments, or the lack of. If there's any trend in the current western hemisphere (and consequently the larger world due to its influence), I believe it would be the greater emphasis on the importance of the individual. This could have the artistic effect where perhaps personal idiosyncrasy is more and more favored. There are certainly myriad more movements/genres now than ever before given the ever-lowering barrier to entry for creation and the ability to reach an audience or find similarly-minded artists. We have covered the obvious extremes of art--craftmanship epitomized in people like Cabanel and the disintegration in Dadaist art and beyond. In doing so, so much of the "in-between" has been missed, enough to perhaps keep us occupied indefinitely. on its origin Art is decidedly derived from the "real world". There are certain appeasing qualities of creative works that immediately may not seem to be representational--that is, it invokes some emotion without an explicit reference to a certain 'thing' in the 'real' world. It's not much of a mystery why looking at a political cartoon might incite some feeling but it's not so apparent how a musical resolution can be moving. And music is an obvious art to focus on, because of how many aspects are not immediately referential. As will be determined, however, each component of a musical work is full of references on references that derive from reality, and that the whole of a piece of music can be determined by enough specificity in intent to be communicated. There can't be any 'purely formal consideration', art is entirely political. Our experience with art is virtually always accompanied by interpersonal signifiers. Their origin is not so complicated: music with a fast beat and loud noises stirs up associations of intensity because it's reminiscent of loud voices, footsteps falling quickly, a fast heartbeat, and so on... There is a very rich body of contextual elements that we take from in the production of a new creative work. Without the cultural context, there is no art, there is no intent. Intent requires a background to work against. This is by definition; a declaration is only meaningful in relation to something else. New words (which come from a need to express a new idea) are necessarily created by concatenation, abbreviation, borrowing a related concept from another field, or any combination of those. Meaning only exists in relation. Older generations are reactionary in aesthetics for this reason--the new elements are challenging, different by their very premise. New sound and visual 'symbols' are created. It has to be different--otherwise there would be no separation. And these symbols exist at every level of abstraction. The musical rondo as a symbol is more complex compared to the examples discussed so far; it's defined as an alternation between 'themes', which are themselves made up of 'motifs' which are made up of notes. And themes and motifs (and notes, but this symbolic idea is so ubiquitous that its being a convention is pretty invisible) all have existing contexts for usage as well as being symbols themselves. Back at the level of the rondo, the 'meaning' from usage of this form has changed over time; first, as something 'new', and associated with contemporary composing, then as an established convention, and now music written in rondo form from those centuries can be considered dated or 'classical' against the background of everything that has occurred since. So the rondo form includes a whole hierarchy of further nested symbols, and can also be used in every symbolic manner itself, just like any 'lower' symbol it implicitly builds from. Our capacity for symbolic interpretation is flexible enough to even extend an appeasing quality directly to the 'fine craftsmanship' of an artwork. Immediately, this may seem to be an 'isolating' of the formal qualities of an artwork; instead of making any reference to the interpersonal collection of ideas, observation and appreciation could be founded entirely on the sensory qualities of the artwork. However, our perception is inundated with symbolic augmentations from our 'culture' down to the lowest level. It's established that sensory input is highly processed by the brain, 'filling in the gaps', so that no observance is untouched. Without a larger background to tap into, art based around these acultural 'observances' could take any shape, which is also to say that they have no shape. is there good and bad art? Yes. If you believe there is, at least. Generally, art is done with achieving some intent or another: the success of a work is simply how well it achieves its goals. In addition to that, these are some common schools of thought that artworks tend to align with, typically as a combination of several rather than just one: - imitationalist - art that reproduces accurately what we see - instrumentalist - art used as a means of guiding social values - formalist - evaluating art by its intrinsic properties such as color or musical pitch - expressionist - art as a means of expressing emotion There's also the question of "technique" or "craft" as a proxy for artistic quality--in drawing there's the obvious path of better perspective and anatomy since typically people want to make art that at least somewhat emulates reality. But the goal in music isn't to recreate naturally occurring sounds, and there's much more to writing fiction than a convincing impression of an environment. And drawing isn't necessarily about making a convincing recreation of something either; it's just that the medium lends itself to that sort of practice much more readily. The real answer is that the success of a work comes down to how well it fulfills the creator's goals. But that's not saying every work has its own merits and can't be considered a poorer creation. Something can fall short of a creator's intention, or maybe they don't even have a clear thing they wanted to communicate in the first place which would severely harm its quality. One implication of this is that you will in fact be faulted for trying to do more and falling short, whereas if you had simply picked a smaller hurdle to try to succeed at the final result may have been much more favorably recieved. However, there's more potential value to be had in more "insightful" or lofty artistic aims. art as craft Although the importance of craft varies in people's taste, it's worth mentioning because of its fairly universal applicability overall. This is because the essence of craft is variation, which is employed in virtually every artistic work. We can determine the basic elements of a medium by considering which qualities everything else derives from in it. Each element is then varied, and these combinations are the basis of good craft. In visual I would call it limited to hue, value and edge. Conventionally it's listed as hue, value, shape, texture, form, line, space, but form and space are easily seen to derive from value and line, given that these are simply two-dimensional illusions. Whether to include line as distinct from shape is not really important and texture is built out of shapes. Finally, a shape is really a value with an edge. Similarly I think music can be distilled to variations in timbre, dynamics, pitch, and rhythm. Anyone can see that they should choose more than one color in general but the concept of using variety becomes more difficult when groups of smaller elements start coming into play--color and value should vary across the surface of an object, but also across a painting as a whole--distinct areas and objects should have different "baseline" colors and values to modulate from. originality Originality is an odd idea. I think it's fairly obvious that "new" creations are always a combination of influences and one's personal inclinations. In order for communication to happen there needs to be some understanding as a starting point. Creativity is just putting that mix of influence and personal idiosyncrasy into something concrete.
