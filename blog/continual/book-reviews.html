07.24.23 | Ellis, American Psycho - 6.5/10 It's rare for a book to get better towards the end, although it might be improved by dropping the first ~35%. We get it, we don't need more mentions of clothing brands or restaurants or whatever to understand the attitudes of these people. There's a certain "living their cyclical, shallow life" aspect that it adds to, but I don't think that effect is lost for brevity. It made a lot more sense for that quote about "simply not being there" to come close to the end of the book rather than in the first five minutes like the movie chose.. Also it made more sense for the detective to never follow up with Bateman (unlike the movie) given all his suspicious answers because it falls in line with the whole idea much better that the people in this community are highly interchangeable; everyone's mistaken for each other and they are all motivated more or less by the same common denominator. Who's to say that Owen was his "real" name anyway? Owen thought he was Marcus, why not the other way around?

12.13.22 | Carr, Is Google Making Us Stupid? - 3/10 The general argument and conclusions presented are sound, but his manner of presentation leaves a bad taste. Ending your essay with a statement like 'as we come to rely on computers to mediate our understanding of the world, it is our own intelligence that flattens into artificial intelligence' is just... cringe. He further says that 'HAL’s outpouring of feeling contrasts with the emotionlessness that characterizes the human figures in the film... In the world of 2001, people have become so machinelike that the most human character turns out to be a machine', which is a really, really big reach. 2001 is about the ascendance of humanity, underscored by Dave's victory over the superintelligent machine HAL. 11.04.22 | Keyes, Flowers for Algernon (short story version) - 3/10 My main thought after reading is how implausible the story felt, and I'm not referring to the sci-fi elements. Before all that, I'll say that I liked the premise a lot, and the general theme of compassion was nice. The entire plot is moved by the changing of his relationships with the other characters, but they just felt... weird. It was odd how Frank and Joe are remorseful about the situation, clearly they never felt bad about their actions prior to Charlie's operation. My next thought is maybe his increase in intelligence humanized him, and in retrospect that understanding caused them guilt. In that case, the petition signed by 840 of the workers makes no sense. Clearly what the author was going for was a sense of disgust or fear from his coworkers. And they certainly would not have welcomed him back in that case. So I really feel like that whole situation could have been much more interesting. I thought the "high intelligence" entries were similarly, well, not so great. In all honesty, it sounds like a not very smart person writing how they imagine a really smart person thinks. One of the parts that bothered me is his thoughts about Nemur and Strauss. Charlie comments about the limits of the two doctors' understanding tactlessly. Now this is actually an interesting point, because what the author could have done was explore the idea of someone who, being suddenly gifted a great amount of intelligence, is still severely lacking in "real world experience", develop that side. But he doesn't pursue that angle and we are left with something more awkward in its ambiguousness. Why is he brusque/tactless with the doctors? If he were extremely intelligent, this should inform his interpersonal observations as well, or otherwise develop in the direction I mentioned earlier. Instead, the author chooses the "quirky genius who is shocked other people aren't as smart", which for one just makes no sense - his intelligence grew over time so he should be aware of the gradual change in perspective and limitations of others. Or does his increase in intelligence make him an amnesiac ? (It doesn't, that's never suggested). Charlie's complete lack of self awareness is further evidenced in his May 18 entry: he writes, "I saw Miss Kinnian last night for the first time in over a week. I tried to avoid all discussions of intellectual concepts and to keep the conversation on a simple, everyday level, but she just stared at me blankly and asked me what I meant about the mathematical variance equivalent in Dorbermann’s Fifth Concerto." The author having already trotted out the stereotypically unaware genius trope, it's pretty straightfoward what he meant by this. That line definitely killed me a bit. Again, I like the premise a lot and I think the general theme of having compassion for others is good, but this story could have been a lot more if someone else had written it. A much more entertaining portrayal of high intelligence and extrapolation to superintelligence is Understand by Ted Chiang.
